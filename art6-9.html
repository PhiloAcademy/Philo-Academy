Art Program: Grades 6-9

The 6-9 Art Program at Philo Classical Academy builds on the K-5 foundation, deepening students’ appreciation of beauty and classical art through projects tied to literature and history. In 6th grade, students explore Ancient Art and Symbolism, creating Mesopotamian reliefs and Greek vases while reflecting on heroism and faith. 7th graders study Medieval Art and Chivalry, crafting illuminated manuscripts and heraldic shields to express honor and community. 8th graders delve into Renaissance and Early Modern Art, using perspective and allegory to explore humanism and satire. 9th graders focus on American Art and Identity, creating Romantic landscapes, Realist portraits, Hudson River School-inspired seascapes, and Modernist works to reflect on morality, nature, and the rejection of classical traditions, preparing them for advanced artistic and cultural analysis in high school.

6th Grade: Ancient Art and Symbolism

Students explore ancient art from Mesopotamia, Egypt, Greece, Rome, and the Islamic Golden Age, focusing on symbolism and the depiction of heroism, faith, and morality. In the first trimester, inspired by "The Epic of Gilgamesh", they create a clay relief of a heroic scene, exploring Mesopotamian techniques and reflecting on beauty in heroism. In the second trimester, tied to "The Odyssey", students paint a paper mache vase with a scene of Odysseus’ journey, using Greek black-figure techniques, discussing balance in art. In the third trimester, inspired by "Tales from the Arabian Nights", they design a tile with Arabic calligraphy and geometric patterns, reflecting on beauty in Islamic art’s symmetry.

Set Piece Art Study: Students reflect on The Standard of Ur (c. 2600 BCE, Mesopotamia), a Sumerian artifact depicting war and peace. They explore how its narrative relief conveys beauty through order and heroism, inspiring their own relief project.

7th Grade: Medieval Art and Chivalry

Students study medieval art, including illuminated manuscripts, stained glass, and Gothic architecture, focusing on chivalry, faith, and community. In the first trimester, inspired by "The Story of King Arthur and His Knights", they create an illuminated manuscript page of Arthur’s court, using gold paint, discussing beauty through faith. In the second trimester, tied to "The Door in the Wall", students design a stained glass window with transparent paper, reflecting on how Gothic art uses light for beauty. In the third trimester, inspired by "The Adventures of Robin Hood", they create a heraldic shield with symbols of justice, exploring beauty through symbolism.

Set Piece Art Study: Students examine The Unicorn in Captivity (c. 1495-1505, South Netherlandish), a medieval tapestry symbolizing purity and chivalry. They discuss how its intricate details reflect beauty through faith and honor, inspiring their manuscript project.

8th Grade: Renaissance and Early Modern Art

Students explore Renaissance and early modern art, focusing on perspective, portraiture, and allegory, emphasizing humanism, virtue, and satire. In the first trimester, inspired by "Romeo and Juliet", they draw a street scene in Verona using one-point perspective, reflecting on Renaissance realism. In the second trimester, tied to "The Faerie Queene", students paint an allegorical self-portrait with symbols of virtue, discussing beauty through meaning. In the third trimester, inspired by "Gulliver’s Travels", they create a satirical cartoon of a fantastical land, exploring how early modern art uses beauty in critique.

Set Piece Art Study: Students analyze The Birth of Venus by Sandro Botticelli (c. 1485, Renaissance Italy), a masterpiece of humanism and beauty. They reflect on how Botticelli’s use of balance and idealism depicts beauty, inspiring their perspective drawing.

9th Grade: American Art and Identity

Students study American art movements like Romanticism, Realism, the Hudson River School, and Modernism (1915–1960), focusing on identity, morality, humanity’s relationship with nature, and the rejection of classical traditions through chaos, deconstruction, and surrealism. In the first trimester, inspired by "The Scarlet Letter", they paint a Romantic landscape symbolizing Puritan isolation, reflecting on beauty in emotion. In the second trimester, students explore Realism and the Hudson River School: tied to "Huck Finn", they create a realist portrait of a character, using charcoal to capture emotion, discussing beauty through truth; and inspired by "The Old Man and the Sea", they create a Hudson River School-inspired seascape using oil pastels and mixed media, depicting Santiago’s struggle with glowing light and expansive skies, focusing on the art of nature and water as a source of spiritual renewal. In the third trimester, tied to "The Great Gatsby", students study Modernist art and create a Picasso-inspired Cubist portrait of a character using collage and acrylics, exploring themes of fragmentation and chaos in the Jazz Age.

Set Piece Art Studies: Students reflect on The Oxbow by Thomas Cole (1836, Hudson River School), exploring how Cole’s use of light and composition captures the spiritual beauty of nature, inspiring their seascape project. They also study Les Demoiselles d’Avignon by Pablo Picasso (1907), a foundational Cubist work that rejects classical beauty for fragmentation and chaos, inspiring their Modernist portrait project.
